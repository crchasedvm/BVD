BVD: The Silent Thief

An overview of Bovine Viral Diarrhoea (BVD) in New Zealand, a costly and complex disease driven by a small number of highly infectious animals.

The Core Problem: PIs

The entire BVD problem is driven by Persistently Infected (PI) animals. These are virus factories, shedding millions of viral particles daily and infecting the herd.

Widespread & Costly

BVD costs the NZ dairy industry an estimated $150 million annually through reproductive loss, reduced milk production, and animal deaths.

Controllable Disease

Unlike many diseases, BVD can be effectively controlled and even eradicated from a farm by identifying and removing the source: the PI animals.

Understanding the PI Threat

A Persistently Infected (PI) animal is not just sick; its body thinks the BVD virus is a normal part of itself. This section explains how these crucial animals are created. The timing of infection during pregnancy is everything.

Clinical Impact & Signs

BVD infection can look like many other diseases, or it can be completely invisible. The impact depends on the animal's age, immune status, and whether a PI is present in the herd. Click the cards to learn more.

Reproductive Failure

This is the biggest cost. Includes abortions, embryonic death (returns to service), stillbirths, and the creation of PI calves. A classic sign is a condensed calving pattern followed by a long tail of late calvers.

Ill Thrift & Poor Growth

Transiently infected animals and PI calves often show poor growth rates, rough coats, and are generally "poor-doers." They are more susceptible to other diseases like pneumonia and scours.

Reduced Milk Production

Cows that are transiently infected can suffer a temporary drop in milk production. The overall herd production is also lowered due to reproductive inefficiency and general poor health.

Congenital Defects

Infection during mid-to-late gestation can lead to calves born with brain damage (e.g., cerebellar hypoplasia, causing them to be "wobbly"), cataracts, or other developmental issues.

Mucosal Disease

A rare but fatal condition that ONLY occurs in PI animals. If a PI gets super-infected with a different BVD strain, it develops severe, untreatable ulcers throughout the gut, leading to rapid death.

Immunosuppression

BVD virus suppresses the immune system, making the entire herd more vulnerable to other common diseases like mastitis, lameness, and respiratory infections. This is a major hidden cost.

Diagnosis & Control Strategy

The BVDfree New Zealand programme provides a clear pathway to control. The strategy is simple: find the PIs, remove them, and keep them out.

The Path to BVD Freedom

1

Screen the Herd

Start with a bulk tank milk test or a blood test on a sample group of unvaccinated youngstock. This gives a "yes" or "no" answer to whether BVD virus is actively circulating in your herd.

2

Find the PI(s)

If the screen is positive, the hunt begins. This involves blood testing individual cows or pooling milk samples to find the one(s) with the virus. Your vet will develop a testing strategy based on your herd setup.

3

Test All Calves

Since PIs are born, not made, every calf must be tested to ensure it's not a new PI. This is easily done with an ear notch tissue sample taken at tagging. This is the most important step for long-term control.

4

Remove PIs Immediately

Any animal confirmed as a PI must be culled as soon as possible. They cannot be treated and will never recover. Keeping them on farm guarantees the BVD problem will continue.

5

Implement a Vaccination Firewall

Once the herd is clear of PIs, vaccination acts as an insurance policy. It protects your breeding animals from infection during the critical pregnancy window, preventing the creation of new PIs if the virus is accidentally reintroduced. Timing is critical to ensure protection during mating.

  • Heifers (R1s/Maidens): Must complete a two-shot primary course before their first mating.
    • Sensitiser (1st shot): Give 6-8 weeks before planned start of mating (PSM).
    • Booster (2nd shot): Give 3-4 weeks before PSM.
  • Cows (Mixed-Age Herd): Require a single annual booster shot.
    • Annual Booster: Give 3-4 weeks before PSM. This is often done at or near drying off to fit with farm schedules.

Keeping BVD Out: Biosecurity

Once your herd is BVD-free, keeping it that way is paramount. Biosecurity is your insurance policy against re-infection.

    Boundary Fences

    The biggest risk is nose-to-nose contact with neighbour's cattle. Double-fencing on boundaries is the gold standard. Ensure all fences are stock-proof.

    Test All Incoming Stock

    Never assume an animal is BVD-free. Any purchased, leased, or returning animal (including bulls) must be tested for BVD and confirmed negative before they join the main herd.

    Strategic Vaccination

    Vaccination acts as a safety net. It won't cure a PI, but it can protect your breeding cows from becoming infected and creating new PIs if the virus is accidentally introduced.
